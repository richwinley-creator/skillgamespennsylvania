"Pigs Get Fat, Hogs Go to Slaughter" — What PA's Budget Hearing Revealed About the 52% Skill Games Tax

March 12, 2026 7 min read

At a Pennsylvania Capitol budget hearing on March 11, Revenue Secretary Pat Browne defended Gov. Shapiro's proposed 52% skill games tax — and admitted there is no market study supporting it. The blunt response from one Republican lawmaker captures exactly why this debate is far from over.

What Happened at the Hearing

The 2026-27 Pennsylvania budget process entered its third week of hearings on Tuesday, March 11. Among the topics before the House Appropriations Committee: the single biggest new revenue line in Governor Josh Shapiro's $53.3 billion spending proposal — a projected $766 million from regulating and taxing skill games at a 52% rate.

Revenue Secretary Pat Browne told lawmakers the 52% rate was "consistent and fair tax policy," drawing a direct comparison to video gaming terminals (VGTs) at truck stops, which are already taxed at the same rate under state law. The logic: if two types of machines behave similarly in the marketplace, they should face the same tax treatment.

State Rep. Jamie Barton, a Republican representing parts of Schuylkill and Berks counties, didn't buy it. He invoked a well-worn Pennsylvania proverb: "Pigs get fat and hogs go to slaughter."

Barton's point was precise. A tax rate set so high that it destroys the revenue base it's supposed to capture isn't smart policy — it's self-defeating. A 52% rate wouldn't just squeeze operators. It would likely cause widespread machine removal, collapsing the very tax base Shapiro is counting on to close a $4.5 billion gap in his budget — a gap being covered, in part, by draining the state's entire Rainy Day Fund.

The Critical Admission: No Market Impact Study Exists

Barton asked Browne directly: had the Revenue Department studied what a 52% tax rate would actually do to the skill games market?

Browne's answer: no such study exists. His department's position, he said, was that the market could "sustain" the proposed rate. That word — sustain — is doing a lot of work in a budget projection worth $766 million.

This is significant. Shapiro's budget assumes a specific revenue yield based on taxing tens of thousands of currently operating machines. But the administration has not modeled what happens to that number if operators pull machines out of low-volume locations because the economics no longer work. There is no elasticity analysis. No threshold study. No modeling of operator behavior under different rate scenarios.

The $766 million figure is a projection built on an assumption — not an analysis.

Who Actually Has Machines in Pennsylvania

Barton's "pigs and hogs" comment wasn't abstract. He was speaking for a constituency — and it's one that doesn't look like a casino floor.

Tens of thousands of skill games operate today in:

  • Small family-owned convenience stores and roadside shops
  • Bars and taverns serving rural and suburban communities
  • VFW posts and American Legion halls using machine revenue to fund veteran programs
  • Volunteer fire companies supplementing dwindling municipal budgets
  • Fraternal organizations and social clubs throughout the commonwealth

For these venues, skill game revenue isn't a luxury — it's a structural part of operating budgets. At 52%, a machine generating $1,500 per month in net revenue would carry roughly $780 in monthly state tax before any revenue share with the location. That's not a workable arrangement for a VFW in Schuylkill County or a bar in rural Berks. Many would simply remove the machines.

That's the dynamic Barton was pointing to. It's also why the Governor's $766 million projection deserves far more scrutiny than it has received.

The Tax Rate Landscape Heading Into Budget Season

The 52% proposal is not the only rate on the table. As of March 2026, Pennsylvania legislators are actively debating at least four distinct approaches:

  • 52% — Governor Shapiro's proposal (same as VGT rate)
  • 33% — A rival Senate Republican bill
  • 16% — Senator Gene Yaw's SB 1079, now with a House companion bill, with oversight assigned to the Department of Revenue rather than the Gaming Control Board
  • $500/machine/month flat fee — A simpler alternative that has been floated in various discussions

The difference between these rates isn't marginal — it's existential for significant portions of the operator community. At 16%, a $1,500/month machine pays roughly $240/month in state tax. At 52%, that same machine pays $780/month. The range between the best and worst outcome for operators is more than $500 per machine, per month.

Multiply that across 60,000 to 80,000 machines statewide, and the stakes come into focus quickly.

The Budget Deadline and What Comes Next

Pennsylvania's constitutionally mandated budget deadline is June 30, 2026. That's the hard wall. A deal on skill games regulation — including the tax rate — needs to be part of the final budget package, or it risks being pushed into another year of limbo while machines continue to operate in a legal gray zone.

The PA Supreme Court is also still in play. The court took up the question of whether skill games constitute gambling devices and heard oral arguments in 2025. A ruling is expected in 2026. If the court rules against skill games before a legislative framework is in place, the entire regulatory picture shifts dramatically.

The combination of a budget deadline, competing legislative proposals, and a pending Supreme Court ruling makes the next 90 to 110 days the most consequential window in the history of Pennsylvania skill games.

What Operators Should Be Doing Right Now

Run the math at each tax scenario.

Pull your per-machine revenue data and model your net at 16%, 33%, and 52%. Know which locations survive each scenario and which don't. This isn't pessimism — it's preparation. If a deal comes in at 33%, you need to know that day one which machines to keep and which routes to restructure.

Make your legislator hear a name and a number.

Revenue Secretary Browne told lawmakers the market could "sustain" 52% without a study. The only way to counter that assumption is to put real numbers in front of real legislators. How many machines do you operate in their district? What is their annual revenue contribution to local venues? How much do those venues contribute to their communities? That data has more credibility than any lobbyist's talking point.

Coordinate with your locations — especially community organizations.

VFW posts, fire companies, and American Legion halls that use skill game revenue for community programs have extraordinary credibility in Harrisburg. They are not casino operators. They are not out-of-state gaming companies. They are constituents. Their voices, delivered directly to their House and Senate representatives, carry weight that can move votes.

Watch the Supreme Court docket.

A ruling against skill games before a regulatory bill passes would create immediate legal uncertainty and could trigger enforcement actions. If the court rules favorably, it strengthens the hand of legislators pushing for a lower rate. Track the docket. Know the timeline.

The Bottom Line

The March 11 hearing confirmed two things. First, Governor Shapiro's administration has a number it wants — $766 million — but no analysis supporting whether that number is actually achievable at a 52% rate. Second, there are legislators willing to say publicly what operators have been saying privately: this rate is punitive, not practical.

The Barton exchange won't be the last pushback. As the budget process moves toward its June 30 deadline, the 52% rate will face increasing pressure from rural and suburban Republicans whose constituents operate and host these machines. The outcome is not predetermined — but the window to shape it is narrowing.

The time to make noise in Harrisburg is now, not after the budget passes.
