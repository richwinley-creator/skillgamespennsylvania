While Harrisburg Debates, Municipalities Are Already Collecting: What PA Skill Games Operators Need to Know

Harrisburg is still arguing about whether to tax skill games at 16%, 33%, or 52%. The PA Supreme Court hasn't issued its ruling yet. But in Sunbury, Pennsylvania, a former police chief just got assigned to physically count every skill games machine in the city — and make sure permit fees are paid. Operators who are waiting for state-level clarity before taking compliance seriously may be running out of time.

$300 Annual permit fee per machine in Sunbury
June 30 PA constitutional budget deadline — state tax framework expected
2 layers Municipal + state — operators may owe fees at both levels

What Happened in Sunbury

At a Sunbury City Council meeting this week, Mayor Josh Brosious announced that former Police Chief Travis Bremigen will lead an audit to determine how many Pennsylvania Skill Games machines are operating throughout the city. The goal: identify all active machines and collect the city's $300-per-unit permit fee from operators who haven't paid.

Councilman Andrew Ramos, who oversees the code department, is coordinating the effort. The mayor offered the direct assistance of the police department to help with the count.

The city's message is clear: Sunbury is not waiting for Harrisburg to act. It has existing local ordinances, a fee structure, and now an enforcement mechanism — a former police chief with institutional knowledge of the city's commercial corridors.

For operators running machines in Sunbury and similar municipalities, that last detail matters. This isn't a code enforcement form being mailed out. It's a proactive, door-to-door audit led by someone who knows exactly which bars, convenience stores, and social clubs have machines in the back.

Why This Is a Larger Trend

Sunbury isn't an outlier. Across Pennsylvania, municipalities have been operating in a regulatory gray zone alongside the state — many have passed local ordinances requiring per-machine permits, annual fees, and location registration well before any state framework existed.

This happened for a straightforward reason: the state has repeatedly failed to regulate skill games at the statutory level. Governor Shapiro's attempts in 2024 and 2025 both fell apart before the budget deadline. With no state framework in place, municipalities filled the gap with local ordinances — and they now have years of institutional practice collecting those fees.

As the June 30 budget deadline approaches and state-level legislation moves closer to passage, local governments have an additional incentive to collect now: once a state framework passes, it may preempt or restructure local fee authority. Cities that haven't collected what's owed may lose that window.

"Municipalities aren't waiting for Harrisburg — they have their own fee structures, their own permit requirements, and now they're assigning people to actually enforce them."

The Two-Layer Compliance Problem

Here's the operational reality most operators need to map out right now:

Level What's Required Status
Municipal Per-machine permit, annual fee (varies by city — $300+ common) Active in many PA municipalities now
State Tax rate (16%–52% under debate), operator license, machine registration Pending — expected by June 30, 2026
Supreme Court Legal classification of skill games (not slot machines) Decision expected 2026 — could reshape both layers

The practical risk: an operator who is compliant at the state level may still owe back-fees at the municipal level. And right now, municipal enforcement is the more immediate exposure — because it's actually happening.

What This Means for Operators on Multi-Location Routes

If you're running machines across multiple municipalities in central or eastern Pennsylvania, this Sunbury situation should prompt a quick audit of your own:

  • Do you have a current permit in every municipality where you operate? Fee structures and permit requirements vary significantly by jurisdiction. Some cities require annual renewal; others are one-time or tied to business license filings.
  • Are your location partners aware of local permit requirements? In some jurisdictions, the fee obligation falls on the location owner rather than the machine operator. That's a potential source of conflict — and a compliance gap — if it's not clearly addressed in your operator agreements.
  • Do you have a record of what you've paid and when? If a city sends an enforcement officer to count machines, the first question is whether you're licensed. You want documentation ready — not a scramble through old emails.

The Supreme Court Factor

Threading through all of this is the Pennsylvania Supreme Court's pending decision. Oral arguments happened in November 2025 — the court heard the Commonwealth's argument that skill games are effectively the same as slot machines, and should therefore be treated identically under existing gambling statutes.

Lower courts ruled in favor of skill games operators, holding that the skill component distinguishes these machines from slot machines. But several justices at oral argument appeared skeptical.

A ruling against skill games would not immediately end the industry — the legislature could still pass a regulatory framework that legalizes and taxes them explicitly. But it would create a gap, likely triggering enforcement actions in the period between a court ruling and any legislative response. Operators in that window face the most exposure.

A ruling in favor of skill games strengthens the hand of legislators pushing for a lower tax rate (the 16% Yaw proposal) by confirming the legal legitimacy of the industry. Either outcome accelerates the state-level regulatory timeline — which in turn affects what happens at the municipal level.

The June 30 Deadline Is Real

Pennsylvania's constitutional budget deadline is not a suggestion. And in 2026, Shapiro has $766 million in projected skill games revenue built into his spending plan. That number disappears if a deal doesn't pass — and he's already drawing down the entire Rainy Day Fund to cover a $4.5 billion gap.

The political and fiscal pressure to reach a deal before June 30 is higher than it has ever been. Operators should assume some form of state framework will pass this year. The open question is what rate it comes in at — and whether the municipal fee layer gets preempted, preserved, or restructured when it does.

What to Do Right Now

Audit your municipal compliance, location by location.

Pull a list of every city, borough, or township where you operate. Call or check online to confirm whether a local permit is required, what the fee is, and whether you're current. This takes a day of administrative work — far less painful than getting a knock from a former police chief.

Get your documentation in order.

For each location: keep a file with the permit receipt, the operator agreement, and the machine serial number. If enforcement comes, you want to be the operator who hands over a clean folder — not the one who says "I think we paid that last year."

Have the conversation with your location partners.

Make sure your location owners know that municipal fees are coming — or already here. If your agreement is ambiguous about who pays local permit fees, clarify it now. A location owner who gets blindsided by a $300 fee (per machine) is a location owner who calls you first and starts reconsidering the arrangement.

Watch the legislative calendar.

The next major milestones are the state budget hearings continuing through April, followed by the June 30 deadline. Any bill that passes will include provisions on municipal fee preemption — or not. That language will determine whether operators face one fee layer or two going forward.

The Bottom Line

The Sunbury story is a small-city news item. But it signals something operators across Pennsylvania should take seriously: enforcement doesn't wait for legislative consensus. Municipalities have fees on the books, some have been under-collecting for years, and a few are now actively closing that gap.

The state framework is coming before June 30. The Supreme Court ruling is coming sometime in 2026. Both will reshape the compliance picture significantly. But right now, today, the most immediate exposure for many operators is a local permit they haven't renewed — or never pulled in the first place.

Get your municipal house in order before the state framework arrives and enforcement gets a lot more systematic.
