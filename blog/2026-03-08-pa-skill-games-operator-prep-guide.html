What PA Skill Game Operators Should Do Right Now While the Supreme Court Case Plays Out

March 8, 2026 By Skill Games Pennsylvania 7 min read

The Pennsylvania Supreme Court is expected to issue its ruling on skill games — potentially determining whether the machines violate the PA Race Horse Development and Gaming Act — sometime in 2026. Legislators can't agree on a tax rate (the gap between the proposed 16% operator-supported rate and the casino-backed 52% is enormous). The result: the entire industry is in a holding pattern. If you're a PA skill game operator, here's how to run your operation intelligently while the dust settles.

Where Things Stand: A Quick Recap

Pennsylvania has an estimated 68,000+ skill game terminals operating across convenience stores, bars, truck stops, and similar venues. These machines generate significant revenue — revenue the state is eager to tax, and that casinos want redirected to their floors.

The PA Supreme Court heard arguments on the core question: do skill games constitute illegal gambling under current state gaming law? The Commonwealth Court previously sided with skill game operators (a major Pace-o-Matic victory), but the Supreme Court's review could go either way.

Meanwhile, the legislature failed to include skill game regulation in the 2025 state budget, with the House pushing a 16% tax and Senate Republicans suggesting 35%. Expect 2026 to be when something finally gives — either through the courts, the legislature, or both.

The Three Possible Outcomes (and What Each Means for You)

Outcome 1: Supreme Court Upholds Skill Games ✅

This is the best-case scenario. A ruling in favor of operators would essentially confirm that skill games don't violate existing gaming law — putting the burden back on the legislature to pass new regulation if they want to act. Likely result: a negotiated tax framework passes in 2026, probably in the 16-35% range, with formal licensing. Operators who are already running clean, documented operations will be positioned to get licensed quickly.

Outcome 2: Supreme Court Rules Against Skill Games ❌

The most disruptive outcome. A ruling that skill games violate the Gaming Act would trigger immediate legal and enforcement pressure. However, a full overnight shutdown of 68,000+ terminals affecting thousands of small businesses is practically and politically difficult to execute. A regulatory transition period is more likely — but operators would need to act fast to preserve machines and revenue.

Outcome 3: Court Punts or Issues Narrow Ruling 🔄

The most likely near-term outcome: the court issues a narrow ruling or remands back to lower courts, kicking the decisive resolution further down the road. This would keep the status quo intact and force the legislature to act. An increasingly revenue-hungry state budget discussion makes some form of legislated solution in 2026 likely.

What Smart Operators Are Doing Right Now

1. Document Everything

If there's ever an enforcement action or licensing transition, operators with clean records get through it. Make sure you have:

  • Written placement agreements with every venue
  • Machine serial numbers, manufacturer documentation, and compliance certifications
  • Revenue records (weekly or monthly) per machine location
  • Business entity documentation (LLC or corporation — not sole proprietor)
  • Any local permits or business licenses already in place

2. Build Your Cash Reserve

If a tax framework passes — even at the more operator-friendly 16% rate — your cash flow math changes. A machine generating $1,500/month gross now nets you less after tax. Do this math now, not when you get a 90-day compliance notice. Build a reserve equal to 2-3 months of projected tax liability so you're not scrambling.

3. Diversify Your Location Portfolio

Don't have all your machines in one venue type or one geographic area. If enforcement or regulatory action hits one category (e.g., machines near schools or in establishments without food service), diversification limits your exposure. Spread across convenience stores, bars, and other venue types.

4. Know Your Venue Owners

The relationship with your venue owners is your first line of defense and your primary asset. Operators with strong, trusting relationships with venue owners can weather transitions — relocation, machine swaps, renegotiated splits — much more easily than those running purely transactional arrangements. Now is the time to strengthen those partnerships.

5. Stay Connected to Industry Groups

Organizations like the Pennsylvania Skill Games Association and Pace-o-Matic's operator network provide the fastest updates when legal status changes. If you're not plugged in, you're the last to know — which could mean the last to act. Subscribe to their newsletters and communication channels.

6. Talk to a Gaming Attorney Now (Not When You Need One)

A brief consultation with a Pennsylvania gaming attorney — before anything changes — gives you a clear picture of your current exposure and a response plan for each possible outcome. That's a few hundred dollars for significant peace of mind. If a ruling drops and you have no plan, you'll be calling an attorney in panic mode competing with hundreds of other operators doing the same.

The Tax Rate Question: What's Actually Fair?

The gap between 16% and 52% isn't just a number — it's the difference between a viable industry and a dead one. Here's the reality:

  • At 16%, most well-run operations remain profitable. Operators can absorb the hit, comply, and continue growing.
  • At 35% (the Senate Republican position), margins tighten significantly. Some marginal locations become unprofitable, leading to fleet contraction.
  • At 52% (the casino lobby's preferred rate), the math doesn't work for the majority of small operators. That's not accidental — casinos want skill games off the market.

Most analysts expect a negotiated rate to land somewhere between 20-35% if the legislature acts. Build your financial models around that range.

The Bottom Line for PA Operators

Uncertainty is uncomfortable, but it's manageable. Pennsylvania skill games aren't going away overnight regardless of how the Supreme Court rules — there are too many machines, too many small businesses, and too much state revenue at stake for an abrupt end. But the legal environment is going to clarify significantly in 2026, and the operators who are prepared will capture the regulated upside. The ones who aren't will be reactive and vulnerable.

Clean documentation, cash reserves, diversified locations, strong venue relationships, and an attorney on speed dial: that's the 2026 operator playbook.
