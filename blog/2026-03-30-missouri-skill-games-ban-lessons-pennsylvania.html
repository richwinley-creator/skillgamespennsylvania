A Federal Judge Just Banned Skill Games in Missouri. Pennsylvania Could Be Next.

In mid-March 2026, U.S. District Judge John Ross ruled that skill games operating in Missouri bars, restaurants, and convenience stores are illegal gaming devices under state law. The machines — manufactured by Torch Electronics, a close parallel to Pace-O-Matic in Pennsylvania — were ordered shut down. Pennsylvania operators should be paying close attention: the legal theory that took down Missouri's market is the same one the Pennsylvania Attorney General is pressing before the state Supreme Court right now.

80,000+ Skill game machines currently operating in Pennsylvania
~93 days Until PA's June 30 constitutional budget deadline
2 Parallel threats: Supreme Court ruling + budget stalemate

What Happened in Missouri

Missouri's skill game operators had operated for years under a legal argument that will sound familiar to anyone following Pennsylvania's debate: the machines require an element of player skill to determine outcomes, distinguishing them from pure slot machines regulated under gambling statutes.

Judge Ross disagreed. His ruling found that the functional reality of the devices — regardless of the skill wrapper — made them illegal gambling machines under Missouri law. Torch Electronics, which manufactured most of Missouri's machines, faced the same core challenge Pace-O-Matic has faced in Pennsylvania: that the "skill" feature is either minimal, defeatable, or cosmetic.

"Often, there is an option to disable the skill feature, which means the games proceed exactly like a slot machine." — Industry analysis of the Missouri ruling, March 2026

That observation cuts to the core of every skill games legal battle in the country. If the skill feature is optional or trivial, the machine is a slot machine. And slot machines in states without explicit authorization are illegal. The casino industry — which pays as much as 50% of gross slot revenue in state taxes — has made exactly this argument in Missouri, Pennsylvania, and a growing list of other states where gray-market machines are eating into their customer base.

Pennsylvania Is Running the Same Race

Pennsylvania's Supreme Court heard oral arguments in November 2025. Justices did not signal comfort with the skill games industry's position — one justice asked directly whether it was permissible to give people the opportunity to gamble outside the state's established gambling statutes. A ruling is expected in 2026, potentially before the June 30 budget deadline.

The Pennsylvania Attorney General's argument is structurally similar to what prevailed in Missouri: skill games are functionally equivalent to slot machines, they should require licensing and regulatory oversight, and the 80,000-plus machines currently operating without that framework are operating illegally.

Lower Pennsylvania courts have sided with Pace-O-Matic in multiple instances — but those decisions are now on appeal to the state's highest court. The Commonwealth Court's favorable rulings for operators do not protect operators if the Supreme Court reverses them.

How Pennsylvania Differs from Missouri — and Why That Matters

Not all skill games battles are identical. Pennsylvania has several factors that create a different risk and opportunity profile than Missouri:

Factor Missouri Pennsylvania
Adjudication level Federal district court ruling (March 2026) State Supreme Court — ruling pending
Legislative path Legislature debating legalization Multiple active bills + budget inclusion pressure
Machine count Smaller gray market 70,000–80,000 machines — massive economic footprint
Governor position Opposed Shapiro wants regulation + revenue (52% tax proposal)
Budget incentive No direct budget link $766M projected revenue in Shapiro's FY27 budget

The most important difference: Pennsylvania's Governor wants skill games revenue. That's a fundamentally different political dynamic than Missouri, where the state's posture was purely enforcement-oriented. Shapiro's budget projects $766 million from regulating skill games. That projection only exists if the machines keep running — under a regulatory framework. A court-driven ban would destroy that revenue line, and legislators know it.

That creates a legislative incentive to pass a regulatory bill before the Supreme Court acts — not after. A signed law creating a licensing and taxation framework could potentially moot the enforcement argument, or at minimum establish a clear legal path forward for operators who come into compliance.

The Dual-Track Risk Every PA Operator Faces

Pennsylvania operators are navigating two parallel timelines simultaneously:

Track 1 — The Supreme Court. The ruling could come any day. A ruling against skill games creates immediate enforcement exposure. Pennsylvania State Police have already conducted seizures; a Supreme Court ruling against the industry would green-light far more aggressive enforcement statewide. There would be no grace period.

Track 2 — The Budget. June 30 is the constitutional deadline. If legislators include skill games regulation in the final budget package, it creates a path to operate legally under whatever tax rate and licensing framework passes. If the budget deadline passes with no skill games bill, operators face another cycle of uncertainty — but now with a Supreme Court ruling potentially on the books.

Missouri's operators had one track to worry about: a court. Pennsylvania's operators have both. The risk is higher, but so is the possibility of a legislative resolution that actually protects the industry.

What Missouri's Ban Means for the National Skill Games Picture

The casino industry has been running a coordinated national strategy against gray-market skill games for years. The American Gaming Association and the Association of Gaming Equipment Manufacturers have both actively supported legal challenges in multiple states. Missouri's ruling is a win for that strategy — and signals that federal courts are not reflexively sympathetic to the skill games argument.

That matters for Pennsylvania because the legal arguments are being watched across the country. A Pennsylvania Supreme Court ruling against Pace-O-Matic would be the biggest single blow to the gray-market skill games industry in the nation, affecting more machines than any other state ruling to date. It would also validate the casino industry's multi-state legal strategy.

Conversely, a favorable Pennsylvania ruling — or a legislative deal that creates a clear regulatory home for skill games — would establish PA as the model for how states can capture tax revenue from this industry while keeping small business operators viable.

What Operators Should Do Before a Ruling Drops

Know your exposure by location.

Map every machine against its host business type and jurisdiction. If a ruling triggers enforcement, certain locations — particularly those near casinos or in districts with active enforcement posture — will face action first. Knowing your exposure ahead of time lets you triage rather than scramble.

Engage your legislators before June 30.

The budget window is the best opportunity to shape outcomes. Legislators in districts with significant skill games operator and host business presence need to hear directly from those operators — not through a trade association, but from their actual constituents. Attach a number: how many machines in their district, what revenue they generate for local businesses, what tax at a workable rate would look like.

Understand the licensing requirements in pending bills.

Both the Yaw bill (16%) and the rival Senate proposal (33%) include licensing frameworks. Get ahead of those requirements now. Know what documentation, compliance, and registration processes you'd need to complete to operate legally under each scenario. Being licensing-ready when a bill passes is a competitive advantage over operators who wait.

Don't assume lower court wins protect you.

Favorable Commonwealth Court rulings have protected Pace-O-Matic and operators in Pennsylvania so far. Missouri's operators also had favorable lower court outcomes before the federal ruling went the other way. The Supreme Court is the authority that matters, and until it rules, the legal foundation under every skill game in Pennsylvania is contested.

The Bottom Line

Missouri's skill games market didn't see the ruling coming in time to prepare. Pennsylvania operators have something Missouri's didn't: visible warning signs, a legislative window, and a Governor who has a financial stake in keeping the machines running under regulation. That combination creates a realistic path to a workable outcome — but only if the industry and its allies move decisively in the next 90 days.

The Missouri ruling is not Pennsylvania's fate. But it is a preview of what happens when courts act before legislatures do. The question for PA operators right now is whether Harrisburg moves first.
