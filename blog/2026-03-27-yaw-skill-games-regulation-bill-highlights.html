Senator Yaw's Skill Games Regulation Bill: $500 Monthly Fee & 50,000 Terminal Cap

Published March 27, 2026 | By Skill Games Pennsylvania

Pennsylvania's skill games landscape is shifting. Senator Gene Yaw (R-Lycoming) has introduced comprehensive legislation that would finally regulate the estimated 40,000–50,000 unregulated skill game terminals currently operating across the state. Here's what you need to know about the bill, how it affects operators, and what comes next.

The Yaw Bill: The Three Core Pillars

Senator Yaw's proposed legislation tackles Pennsylvania's largest regulatory gap with three decisive moves:

1. Monthly Terminal Fee: $500 Per Unit

Each skill game terminal would be assessed a $500 monthly licensing fee. For operators with 10 terminals, that's $5,000/month ($60,000/year). For larger deployments of 50 units, expect $300,000 annually just in fees—before game revenue splits.

This fee structure prioritizes profitability: only games generating consistent revenue can absorb these costs, which naturally filters out low-performing locations.

2. Statewide Terminal Cap: 50,000 Units

The legislation would cap Pennsylvania's total skill game footprint at 50,000 terminals statewide. This ceiling:

  • Creates scarcity and protects existing operators from unlimited market saturation
  • Prevents the uncontrolled expansion we've seen in other states
  • Gives operators certainty that their locations won't be flooded with new competition

If Pennsylvania already has 40,000–50,000 terminals, this cap essentially freezes the market—a major win for established operators and their venue partners.

3. State Revenue: $300 Million Annually

The monthly fees ($500 × 50,000 terminals × 12 months) generate approximately $300 million in annual revenue for the Commonwealth. This substantial figure has convinced many legislators that regulation—not prohibition—is the path forward. No state government can ignore $300M/year from an existing market.

Additional Protections in the Bill

Beyond the three pillars, Senator Yaw's legislation includes safeguards for venues and the public:

  • Valid ID Requirement: Players must present ID to operate any skill game, similar to sports betting age restrictions.
  • Machine Limits Per Establishment: Venues can't turn into arcade parlors. There's a cap on machines per location, protecting the character of taverns, fraternal clubs, and small businesses.
  • Unlicensed Game Penalties: Operators running skill games without a license face strengthened criminal and civil penalties. This effectively shuts down illegal operators and protects regulated ones from unfair competition.
  • Local Health & Safety Authority: Municipalities can establish their own health and safety rules for venues hosting skill games, allowing local governments to manage their communities while respecting state regulation.

Why This Matters for PA Operators

For the past five years, Pennsylvania's skill games industry has operated in a legal gray area—profitable but uncertain. The Yaw bill changes that:

  • Legal Certainty: Regulated skill games are not gambling. Courts have ruled that outcomes depend on player skill, not chance. This bill codifies that reality and protects operators from legal challenge.
  • Protected Market: The 50,000 terminal cap and licensing fee create barriers to entry that protect your investment. Fly-by-night operators can't undercut you if they can't afford the license.
  • Venue Loyalty: With a licensing structure in place, venue owners know they're partnering with legitimate operations. This reduces venue churn and increases long-term location stability.

What Happens Next?

Senator Yaw's bill is currently circulating for co-sponsorships in the Pennsylvania Senate. Key steps ahead:

  1. Co-sponsorship Phase: Legislative staff are building support. The more co-sponsors, the stronger the signal to leadership.
  2. Committee Consideration: Once co-sponsorships are finalized, the bill moves to the appropriate Senate committee (likely Community, Economic and Recreational Development).
  3. Budget Negotiations: The $300M revenue projection makes this attractive during budget season. Expect this to become part of broader revenue discussions.
  4. House Companion: A House version will likely emerge in parallel. The two chambers will negotiate final language.

Timeline: Realistically, we're looking at late 2026 or early 2027 for passage—but momentum is building.

Bottom Line

The Yaw bill represents the most serious regulatory push yet in Pennsylvania. It's not a question of whether skill games will be regulated, but when and how much. This bill offers clarity, protection, and a sustainable path forward for operators while generating massive revenue for the state.
