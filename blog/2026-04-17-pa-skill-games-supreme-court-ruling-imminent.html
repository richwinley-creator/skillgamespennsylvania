PA Supreme Court Skill Games Ruling Is Coming — What Operators Need to Know Now

The Pennsylvania Supreme Court heard oral arguments last November. A ruling is expected sometime in 2026. With roughly 70,000 skill game machines operating across the state without a formal regulatory framework, that decision — whenever it lands — will reshape the industry overnight. Here's where things stand, what the possible outcomes mean, and how to position yourself before the ruling drops.

70,000 Unregulated machines operating in PA
7–0 Lower court ruling in favor of skill games (2023)
$1.1B Gov. Shapiro's projected skill games tax revenue
52% Proposed tax rate under Shapiro's budget plan

How We Got Here

This case traces back to a 7–0 Commonwealth Court ruling in December 2023 that found skill games do not constitute gambling under Pennsylvania law because they involve a genuine skill component — specifically, a player's ability to recognize patterns and influence outcomes. That ruling was a decisive win for operators.

The Commonwealth appealed, and the Pennsylvania Supreme Court agreed to take the case. In November 2025, justices heard arguments from both sides. The state's core position: the skill element is incidental, and these machines function like unlicensed slot machines. Operators and their legal teams countered that the pattern-recognition mechanic is substantive — the same distinction the lower court accepted unanimously.

"Lower courts have ruled that skill games are not illegal because they involve a skill component, distinguishing them from slot machines. The Commonwealth argued in the Supreme Court that they are the same as slot machines." — ABC27, November 2025

The Three Possible Outcomes

The Supreme Court's ruling will fall into one of three broad buckets, each carrying distinct consequences for Pennsylvania operators:

Scenario What It Means Operator Impact
Court upholds lower ruling Skill games remain legal; pressure shifts to legislature to regulate Favorable — continue operations, prepare for licensing framework
Court reverses — games classified as gambling Machines must comply with PA Gaming Act; most current operators won't qualify immediately High disruption — compliance window uncertain, potential enforcement risk
Narrow/mixed ruling Court draws a distinction based on machine type or payout mechanism Moderate — some machines survive, others may not; legal uncertainty continues

The Legislative Track Running in Parallel

Regardless of how the court rules, Harrisburg isn't waiting. Representative Ben Waxman (D-Philadelphia) is preparing to introduce the Skill Game Consumer Protection Act, which would establish a formal regulatory framework for skill games if they're eventually permitted to operate.

Key provisions under Waxman's proposal include:

  • Centralized monitoring via the Pennsylvania Gaming Control Board
  • Play-speed limits and cooling-off periods
  • Loss limits and a state-run self-exclusion program
  • Identity verification at retail locations for winnings redemption
  • A portion of tax revenue directed to problem gambling programs

This bill doesn't legalize anything — it's a framework proposal for if and when the legislature acts. But its introduction signals that lawmakers on both sides of the aisle are treating regulation as an inevitability, not a question mark.

"This legislation reflects a simple principle: if skill games are going to be regulated, they should be designed to minimize harm." — Rep. Ben Waxman, April 2026

Why the Casino and Lottery Lobby Is Pushing Hard

Don't underestimate how aggressively the opposition is working this. Pennsylvania's licensed casinos and the state lottery have both lobbied against skill games, citing declining revenue they attribute directly to the machines' proliferation. Casino slot earnings have dipped in recent years. Lottery sales have fallen more sharply.

This isn't an abstract legal debate for them — it's a market share fight. Their argument in court and in Harrisburg is consistent: skill games deliver a gambling experience without the regulatory overhead, consumer protections, or tax obligations that licensed operators carry. That framing resonates with some lawmakers, which is why the tax rate debate has been so contentious. Governor Shapiro's proposed 52% rate — criticized as punitive — reflects at least some accommodation of casino-lobby pressure to make skill games less economically attractive than their licensed competitors.

What Operators Should Do Before the Ruling Drops

The timing is unknown. It could be weeks or it could be later in 2026. That uncertainty cuts both ways — it creates anxiety, but it also creates a window to prepare. Operators who treat this as a planning deadline rather than a moment of paralysis will be in a significantly stronger position.

Document your revenue per location. If regulation comes with licensing requirements tied to demonstrated business viability, clean financials matter. Run the numbers now, not under pressure.

Understand what "compliance" would require. If the court rules against operators, the practical question becomes: what does a path to compliance look like, and what's the timeline? The Waxman bill is one proxy for what that framework might look like. Read it.

Diversify location relationships. Small businesses — convenience stores, taverns, VFWs, social clubs — are the backbone of the PA skill games ecosystem. Those relationships are your operating infrastructure. Strengthen them before any disruption, not after.

Follow the legislative track, not just the court. Even if the Supreme Court rules unfavorably, the legislature has the authority to create a statutory framework that permits operation under defined rules. Governor Shapiro has signaled openness to a tax-and-regulate approach. That path remains open regardless of the court's decision.

The Bottom Line

Pennsylvania's skill games industry is at its most consequential legal inflection point since the 2023 lower court ruling. The Supreme Court decision will set the baseline. The legislative response will determine what the operating environment actually looks like. Neither track is resolved — and both matter.

Operators who stay informed and move proactively on compliance preparation will be best positioned to navigate whatever comes next. Those who assume the status quo holds indefinitely are taking a real risk.
