PA Skill Games Tax Debate: 52% vs. 16% — What It Means for Operators

After years of failed attempts, Pennsylvania lawmakers are making another push to regulate and tax skill games in 2026. Two competing proposals on the table would impact operator revenue dramatically—and in very different ways.

2026: The Year Pennsylvania Finally Acts?

Pennsylvania has been trying to regulate skill games since at least 2019. Year after year, competing interests—casino lobbying groups, small business advocates, veteran organizations, and lottery defenders—have blocked a consensus. But 2026 feels different.

As of early March, there are active competing proposals in both chambers of the General Assembly. Lawmakers who spoke to the Erie Times-News this week said they believe regulation is no longer a question of if, but how. The central fight: what tax rate applies to skill game proceeds.

The two main camps: 52% vs. 16%. The gap is enormous—and which one becomes law will determine whether skill games remain viable for small operators or get priced out of existence.

The 52% Proposal: What Casino Interests Want

The higher-tax proposal—backed by the casino and licensed gaming lobby—would cap the combined number of video gaming terminals (VGTs) and skill game machines at 40,000 statewide and impose a 52% tax on gross revenue.

Proponents argue this rate:

  • Aligns with Pennsylvania's existing slot machine tax structure
  • Generates significant state revenue for education and other programs
  • Creates a "level playing field" with licensed casinos paying similar rates

Critics—especially small business owners and veteran organizations—call it a machine kill. At 52%, most operators running 1–5 machines in a bar or convenience store would see margins evaporate. The tax effectively benefits large, well-capitalized operators who can absorb the hit, while forcing out the small-town bar or laundromat owner.

The 16% Proposal: What Small Operators and Veterans Want

SB 626, championed by Sen. Gene Yaw (R-23) and backed by small business and veterans' groups, takes a very different approach. It proposes:

  • A 16% tax on skill game proceeds
  • A regulatory framework focused on licensing and compliance rather than heavy taxation
  • Protection for small businesses and veterans' organizations that rely on skill game revenue

The 16% rate is more comparable to how Pennsylvania taxes lottery proceeds that flow to seniors' programs. Supporters say it's sustainable for small operators while still generating meaningful state revenue.

The Numbers: What Each Rate Means for Your Business

Let's run the math on a typical Pennsylvania skill game operation—say, 3 machines in a bar generating $3,000/month in combined gross revenue:

Scenario Monthly Revenue Tax (52%) Tax (16%) Net (52%) Net (16%)
3 machines, low volume $3,000 $1,560 $480 $1,440 $2,520
5 machines, medium volume $6,000 $3,120 $960 $2,880 $5,040
10 machines, high volume $12,000 $6,240 $1,920 $5,760 $10,080

Note: Net figures above are pre-location split, machine costs, and operating expenses. For small operators with location splits of 30–40%, the 52% tax scenario leaves very little margin—or none at all.

HB 1141: The House's Approach

HB 1141, currently moving through the House, takes yet another angle. It would establish a formal "local gaming terminal" framework under Title 4 (Amusements), creating a licensing structure, local gaming fund, and compliance enforcement system. The tax rate in this version lands closer to the middle of the two proposals.

The significance of HB 1141 is structural: it treats skill games as a distinct regulated category rather than shoehorning them into existing casino or lottery frameworks. Many operators prefer this approach because it provides clarity and legitimacy without the crushing tax burden of the 52% model.

What Philadelphia Lawmakers Are Saying

Philadelphia-area legislators have been among the most vocal about the unregulated skill games market. Their concern: without a regulatory framework, some locations operate as de facto illegal gaming hubs with no oversight, age verification, or consumer protections.

Multiple Philadelphia lawmakers told Spotlight PA they "hope the legislature will finally address skill games regulations in 2026." Their support for some version of regulation is likely a prerequisite for getting any bill across the finish line, given the city's bloc of House votes.

What Operators Should Do Right Now

Regardless of which proposal wins, regulation is coming. Here's how to prepare:

  1. Document everything now. Revenue records, machine logs, location agreements—get your paperwork organized before any licensing framework kicks in. Clean records make licensing faster and cheaper.
  2. Know your machines. Licensing frameworks typically require manufacturer certification. Make sure the machines you're running are from compliant manufacturers (Pace-O-Matic, Banilla, and other established names are well-positioned for licensing).
  3. Model your business at 52%. It's the worst case. If your current operation is profitable at 52%, you're fine under any scenario. If not, think about volume, machine count, or location quality improvements now.
  4. Engage your legislators. The skill games tax fight is not over. Small business owners contacting their state rep and senator directly has an outsized effect on votes like this. The casino lobby has money; operators have constituents.
  5. Don't panic-expand or panic-contract. Wait for the final bill text before making major business decisions. The range of outcomes is still wide.

Frequently Asked Questions

When will Pennsylvania pass skill games legislation?

No firm timeline exists, but 2026 is the most credible window in years. Multiple active bills in both chambers, cross-party pressure, and budget revenue needs are creating alignment that hasn't existed before. That said, Pennsylvania has a history of skill games legislation stalling—stay informed and don't count on a specific date.

Will existing machines be grandfathered in?

Most proposals include a licensing and registration process for existing machines. Operators with properly manufactured, currently operating machines from recognized manufacturers are expected to have a path to compliance. Unlicensed or non-compliant machines are a different story.

Does a 52% tax make skill games unviable?

For small operators with low-volume placements, yes—it likely does. High-volume operators and larger route operators with many machines can absorb the rate more easily. This is precisely why small business advocates are fighting hard for the 16% framework.

How does PA compare to other states?

Virginia passed skill games legislation and has worked through implementation challenges. Illinois has a mature Video Gaming Terminal (VGT) framework with a tax rate around 34% for the state. Pennsylvania's 52% proposal would make it one of the highest-taxed skill/VGT markets in the country.
