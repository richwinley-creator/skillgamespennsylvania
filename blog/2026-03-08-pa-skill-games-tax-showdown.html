PA Skill Games Tax Showdown: 52% vs. Operators — What 2026 Means for Your Bottom Line

By Skill Games Pennsylvania • March 8, 2026 • 8 min read

Pennsylvania's skill games industry is heading into the most consequential legislative stretch in its history. Governor Josh Shapiro has again called for a 52% tax on skill game proceeds — a rate that would reshape the economics of every terminal in the state. Meanwhile, a Senate counter-proposal circulating at 16% offers a dramatically different path. With the Pennsylvania Supreme Court still weighing in and roughly 80,000 machines currently operating, the stakes could not be higher for operators.

Where Things Stand: Shapiro's 2026-27 Budget Proposal

In his February 3, 2026 budget address, Governor Shapiro renewed his push to tax and regulate skill games at the same rate applied to Pennsylvania casinos: 52% of gross terminal revenue (GTR). His administration projects this would generate approximately $2 billion annually for the commonwealth — with over $1.1 billion per year entering state coffers once the industry reaches a proposed cap of 40,000 combined skill game and video gaming terminal (VGT) machines.

This is the second consecutive year Shapiro has proposed the 52% figure. Last year it stalled in the legislature. This year, with the Supreme Court case still unresolved and the governor increasingly vocal, the pressure on lawmakers to act is meaningfully higher.

The Independent Fiscal Office (IFO) released its own analysis, estimating each skill game machine generates roughly $58,000 in gross revenue annually in the first year. At 52%, the tax burden per machine would approach $30,000 per year. Analysts expect operators and location hosts to absorb roughly $905 million of the industry-wide tax load, with an estimated $370 million effectively passed on to players via lower payouts and higher food and beverage prices at host locations.

The Legislative Counter: A Lower Rate with Guardrails

Not everyone in Harrisburg is on board with the governor's rate. Senator Gene Yaw has been among the most prominent voices pushing for a materially lower tax — reportedly in the range of 16% of gross terminal revenue. His argument: a punishing rate doesn't just cut into margins, it potentially drives machines underground or across state lines, producing less revenue than a more moderate rate would.

The Senate Republican caucus has historically favored a rate closer to 35%, as reflected in earlier proposals like SB 756. The 16% figure represents the lower bound of what's been floated. Where any final bill lands will depend heavily on negotiations between the Republican-controlled Senate, the Democratic-led House, and the governor's office.

The 40,000 Machine Cap: A Hidden Risk for Operators

Lost in the tax rate debate is a provision that deserves at least equal attention: Shapiro's proposal would cap the total number of VGTs and skill games combined at 40,000 machines statewide. Pennsylvania currently operates an estimated 80,000 skill games alone — not counting the VGTs already regulated at truck stops.

If enacted as proposed, this cap would effectively force roughly half of the machines currently operating to come offline. Operators and location owners who don't secure a license under the new regulatory framework would need to exit the market. This creates a significant first-mover dynamic: early compliance could mean a protected operating license; delay could mean exclusion.

The Supreme Court Wildcard

Any legislative analysis has to account for the Pennsylvania Supreme Court, which heard oral arguments on the legality of skill games in November 2025. The court is weighing two core questions:

  • Does the presence of a skill component — even a minimal one — legally distinguish these machines from slot machines?
  • Should existing slot-machine laws be interpreted to cover skill game devices?

Lower courts, including the Commonwealth Court, have consistently ruled in favor of skill game operators, finding the games are games of skill rather than gambling devices. The Supreme Court's ruling — expected sometime in 2026 — could either validate that framework or fundamentally upend it.

If the Court rules against operators, the games could be classified as illegal slot machines under current law, bypassing the legislative tax debate entirely and triggering immediate enforcement. If the Court upholds the lower court rulings, it shifts the action entirely back to the legislature — likely accelerating the push for a regulatory and tax framework that both sides can live with.

What This Means for PA Skill Game Operators: Scenario Breakdown

Scenario A: 52% Tax Passes

This is the governor's preferred outcome and the scenario most aligned with casino industry lobbying. At this rate, a terminal generating $58,000 in GTR would produce roughly $28,000 in annual tax liability. Operators running revenue-share models would see their effective margins compress significantly. Some marginal locations — lower-traffic bars, smaller convenience stores — may no longer be economically viable. Expect consolidation among operators and pressure to renegotiate revenue-share terms with location hosts.

Scenario B: 16%–35% Tax Passes

A negotiated rate in this range keeps the industry viable and likely brings most existing operators into a licensed framework willingly. At 16%, the annual tax on a $58,000-GTR machine would be roughly $9,300 — manageable within existing revenue-share structures. This scenario incentivizes compliance and likely produces a stable, growing licensed industry. It's the best realistic outcome for operators.

Scenario C: Supreme Court Invalidates Skill Games

The worst-case scenario. If the court rules these machines are illegal gambling devices, operators face immediate legal exposure regardless of any tax deal in the legislature. This scenario argues strongly for operators to quietly maintain good relationships with host locations and legal counsel, while staying engaged with the legislative process as a hedge.

What Operators Should Do Right Now

The window to influence the outcome is open — but not indefinitely. Here's a practical action checklist for Pennsylvania skill game operators in the current environment:

  1. Engage your state legislators directly. Call your State Senator and State Representative. Industry associations like the Pennsylvania Skill Games Association are coordinating outreach — connect with them if you haven't already.
  2. Audit your location portfolio. Identify which locations would remain profitable under a 52% tax scenario, and which would not. Know your numbers before any deal is struck.
  3. Review your contracts. Revenue-share agreements signed before this legislative environment may need renegotiation regardless of what rate passes. Build flexibility into renewals now.
  4. Prepare your compliance documentation. Any regulatory framework will require licensing. Start gathering business entity documents, location permits, and machine records so you can move quickly when the window opens.
  5. Monitor the Supreme Court docket. A ruling could come at any time. Have a legal contingency plan in place.

The Bottom Line

Pennsylvania's skill games industry is at an inflection point. The status quo — 80,000 machines operating in a legal gray zone — is almost certainly not where things end up. The question is whether regulation arrives on terms that keep the industry alive and growing, or on terms that gut operator margins and force a reckoning.

A 52% tax at casino parity would be a serious blow. A negotiated 16–35% rate with a clear licensing path would be manageable and potentially stabilizing. The outcome depends on how effectively operators and their allies make their case in Harrisburg between now and the budget deadline.

Stay current on developments, keep your legislators' phone numbers handy, and run your numbers. The operators who come out of this strongest will be the ones who were prepared for every scenario.
