Two Clocks Are Ticking for PA Skill Game Operators: The Supreme Court and the Budget Deadline

Pennsylvania skill game operators are caught between two converging pressure points in spring 2026: a Pennsylvania Supreme Court ruling that could redefine the legal status of the machines entirely, and a June 30 state budget deadline that is forcing lawmakers to finally put a tax number on the table. Both could land within weeks of each other. Neither outcome is guaranteed. And operators who wait for certainty before preparing are already behind.

The Supreme Court Case: What It Actually Decides

Arguments were heard by the Pennsylvania Supreme Court in late November 2025. The central question: are skill games — the touchscreen terminals found in thousands of bars, convenience stores, VFWs, and American Legions across the state — gambling devices under existing law, or something categorically different because of the skill element?

Lower courts, including the Commonwealth Court, ruled in favor of operators. They found that the "Follow Me" pattern-recognition feature constitutes genuine skill, distinguishing these machines from regulated slot machines. The Commonwealth appealed, arguing the skill element is cosmetic — that these devices function like slots and should be regulated as such.

The Supreme Court's ruling will either:

  • Uphold the lower court — skill games remain in a legal gray area, continuing to operate while the legislature debates a framework.
  • Overturn and reclassify — skill games become gambling devices, instantly subject to Pennsylvania Gaming Control Board oversight that most operators cannot currently meet. Machines could face removal or seizure pending compliance.
"Should the court classify skill games as gambling devices, lawmakers would be compelled to either regulate them under state gaming laws or ban them altogether. A decision favoring manufacturers could leave lawmakers with the dilemma of whether to act at all." — NewsNet5, December 2025

No ruling date has been announced. Based on typical timelines after oral arguments, a decision could arrive any time between now and mid-summer. Given that it intersects directly with budget negotiations, the court may move earlier rather than later.

The Budget Fight: 52% vs 16% vs Flat Fee

Governor Josh Shapiro's 2026–27 budget proposal, delivered in February, includes $766 million in projected revenue from regulating and taxing skill games — more than his marijuana legalization proposal is expected to generate. That number only works if the machines are taxed at 52% of gross revenue, the same rate applied to video gaming terminals (VGTs) in truck stops.

The competing proposals on the table:

Proposal Tax Rate / Structure Operator Impact
Shapiro Budget Plan 52% of gross revenue + 40,000 machine cap (combined VGT + skill) Projected to cut small venue revenue significantly; no market study conducted
SB 626 (Senate proposal) 16% of gross revenue + structured licensing fees More sustainable for small operators; lower state revenue yield
Flat Fee Option $500/machine/month Predictable for high-revenue locations; punishing for low-traffic venues

At a March budget hearing, Revenue Secretary Pat Browne defended the 52% rate as "consistent and fair" because it matches what VGTs already pay. State Rep. Jamie Barton pushed back hard, quoting the adage "pigs get fat, hogs go to slaughter" — and noting that Browne's department had conducted no study on what a 52% rate would actually do to the market.

That gap — a tax rate set based on comparability rather than economic modeling — is exactly what operators should be paying attention to. If lawmakers accept the 52% rate without independent analysis, the revenue projections may prove wildly optimistic, and a wave of machine removals from marginal locations would follow.

How the Two Clocks Interact

Here's the dynamic that makes this spring uniquely high-stakes: the Supreme Court ruling and the budget deadline are on a collision course.

If the court rules before the budget passes, it either removes uncertainty (operators survive legally, debate shifts purely to tax structure) or creates an emergency (machines must comply with gaming regulations within a compressed timeline, budget projections collapse, Harrisburg scrambles).

If the budget passes before the court rules, legislators will have locked in a regulatory framework that could be rendered moot or contradicted by the court's decision. That scenario could trigger immediate legal challenges to whatever framework was enacted.

Neither scenario is clean. Both require operators to be prepared rather than reactive.

What Operators Should Be Doing Right Now

Waiting for a final answer is not a strategy. Here's what smart operators are doing in this window:

  • Run your numbers at 52%, 35%, and 16%. Know your break-even at each rate per machine, per location. If 52% makes a venue unprofitable, identify that now — not when the law passes.
  • Audit your machine agreements. Revenue-share vs. lease arrangements have very different tax implications depending on who is the "operator of record" under whatever framework emerges.
  • Get compliant on the basics. Regardless of the court's ruling, any legislative framework will require licensing documentation, location agreements, and machine certification records. Start organizing those files.
  • Talk to your venues. Small business owners — bars, VFWs, fire companies — may not know what's coming. Operators who keep their venue partners informed build loyalty that survives regulatory disruption.
  • Stay close to SB 626. The 16% Senate proposal is the operator-friendly path. If it advances in committee, it will move fast. Be ready to support it, contact your legislators, or both.
"Skill games are a part of the revenue of a lot of businesses. Small family businesses. Fire companies. VFW's. Restaurants. American Legions." — State Rep. Jamie Barton, March 2026 budget hearing

The Bottom Line

Pennsylvania's skill games debate has been running for years. What's different in 2026 is that both tracks — the court case and the budget process — are converging simultaneously, with real deadlines attached. The industry will not be in limbo forever. A ruling and a law are both coming.

Operators who have modeled their exposure, organized their documentation, and built relationships with their venue partners will be in a position to adapt quickly. Operators who are waiting for clarity before they act may find the window to adapt has already closed.
