Missouri's Skill Games Ban Is a Warning for Pennsylvania Operators

A federal judge in Missouri just ruled that Torch Electronics skill game terminals are illegal gaming devices under state law. It's a win for casinos and the regulated gaming industry — and a direct warning for Pennsylvania, where roughly 80,000 machines are still operating under a cloud of unresolved legal and legislative uncertainty. PA's Supreme Court ruling is still pending. The budget clock is ticking. Operators who aren't paying attention could be caught off guard.

80,000+ Skill game machines operating in PA retail locations
2026 Expected year for PA Supreme Court ruling
June 30 PA constitutional budget deadline — skill games tax in play

What Happened in Missouri

In early March 2026, a federal judge sided with Missouri regulators and ruled that Torch Electronics' skill game terminals are illegal gaming devices under state law. The case follows the same legal fault line that's playing out across multiple states: manufacturers claim their machines are legal because winning depends — at least in part — on a player's skill. Courts in some states have agreed; in Missouri, the court did not.

The ruling is significant beyond Missouri's borders. The American Gaming Association and the Association of Gaming Equipment Manufacturers have been pushing coordinated campaigns to eliminate unregulated skill games in multiple states. Missouri is their first clean courtroom victory in this cycle. Pennsylvania is their biggest remaining target.

"In short, the 'skill games' are exploiting a gray area in the law to rake in gambling profits without having to bother with licensing, player protections or state taxes." — Casino Center analysis, March 2026

That framing is exactly the narrative that will land in front of Pennsylvania's Supreme Court justices. Operators should understand how that story gets told — and what's actually at stake in the PA case.

Where Pennsylvania Stands Right Now

Pennsylvania is the largest skill games market in the country. Pace-O-Matic machines are embedded in convenience stores, bars, restaurants, American Legions, and VFW halls across the state. The industry has argued — and lower courts have agreed — that these machines are legal because the "skill-based" memory component allows a player to convert a losing result into a win, distinguishing them from pure-chance slot machines.

But Pennsylvania's Attorney General escalated past those lower court rulings. The AG appealed directly to the state Supreme Court, asking the justices to declare all skill games banned as illegal slot machines. Arguments were heard in November 2025. A ruling is expected sometime in 2026.

The stakes couldn't be higher. If the Supreme Court sides with the AG, it could force a rapid shutdown of operations statewide — or push the legislature to act fast on a regulatory framework to avoid that outcome. If the court sides with the manufacturers, the legislature will still face intense pressure to bring the industry under formal regulation and taxation as part of the 2026-27 budget process.

The Legislative Track: Two Very Different Paths

Regardless of how the court rules, Harrisburg is not done with this issue. Governor Shapiro's 2026-27 budget proposal includes a projected $766 million in new revenue from a 52% tax on skill game gross receipts. That number is central to plugging a $4.5 billion budget gap — and it's driving legislative urgency in a way that previous sessions haven't seen.

The two competing proposals break down like this:

Proposal Tax Rate Machine Cap Status
Shapiro Budget Proposal 52% GGR 5 per location Proposed — not enacted
Industry-Backed Legislation 16% GGR 5 per location Proposed — not enacted
Current Status 0% (unregulated) No limit Operating under lower court rulings

Neither bill has been signed into law. The 52% rate — which Rep. Jamie Barton called out as potentially "self-defeating" at a March 11 budget hearing — remains the governor's opening position. The industry's 16% counter would generate less immediate revenue but is more likely to sustain a viable market long-term. The June 30 budget deadline creates real pressure to resolve this before fall.

Why Missouri Matters for the PA Court Case

The Missouri ruling doesn't bind Pennsylvania courts — state law varies, and the two cases involve different machines and different statutory frameworks. But it does two things that Pennsylvania operators should not ignore.

First, it hands opponents of skill games a fresh, high-profile data point. The AG's argument before the PA Supreme Court — that skill games are functionally identical to slot machines — just got a court ruling in another state that says exactly that. That's not precedent in PA, but it's narrative momentum.

Second, it signals that the casino industry and gaming regulators are playing a long game. Missouri isn't an isolated incident — it's part of a coordinated multi-state push. Pennsylvania, as the largest market, is the crown jewel in that campaign.

What Operators Should Be Doing Now

The machines are still legal under Pennsylvania law today. Lower courts have ruled in the industry's favor. That's the operative reality. But smart operators are not treating that as a reason to stand pat. Here's what preparation looks like right now:

  • Track the legislative calendar. The June 30 budget deadline is the most likely forcing function for a regulatory framework. Follow the House and Senate Appropriations Committee schedules closely.
  • Model both tax scenarios. Run your unit economics at 16% GGR and at 52% GGR. Know your break-even. Know how many machines you can operate profitably at each rate before the number becomes academic.
  • Maintain operator documentation. Any regulatory framework that passes will require location agreements, operator registrations, and potentially background checks. Being organized now puts you ahead of the compliance curve.
  • Engage your trade associations. Pace-O-Matic and Pennsylvania skill games operators have an active lobbying presence in Harrisburg. The 16% counter-proposal didn't materialize out of nowhere — it came from organized industry advocacy. Stay connected.
  • Watch the Supreme Court docket. The ruling could come at any time. Have a contingency plan for the adverse scenario, even if you don't expect it.

The Bottom Line

Pennsylvania is not Missouri. The legal history is different, the machines are different (Pace-O-Matic vs. Torch Electronics), and the lower court rulings have consistently favored operators. But the national landscape is shifting. Missouri's ban is the latest signal that the gray area these machines operate in is getting smaller — not larger.

The best outcome for PA operators is a regulated framework at a sustainable tax rate. That outcome requires the legislature to act before the Supreme Court forces their hand, and it requires the industry to stay engaged through the budget process. The June 30 deadline makes this the most consequential few months for Pennsylvania skill games since the industry took root here.

The machines are still running. Keep them running — with a plan for what happens next.
