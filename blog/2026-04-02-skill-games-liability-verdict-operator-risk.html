The $15.3 Million Verdict PA Skill Game Operators Can't Ignore

While Harrisburg argues about tax rates and the Pennsylvania Supreme Court weighs its skill games ruling, a quieter legal threat has been building on a different front — one that doesn't require a new law to destroy your business. In November 2025, a Pennsylvania jury issued a $15.3 million verdict against a convenience store in Hazelton that hosted skill games machines. No PGCB license. No mandated security. No mandated surveillance. Just machines, cash, and a tragedy that cost a clerk his life.

The verdict arrived days before the Pennsylvania Supreme Court heard oral arguments on whether skill games are legal at all. The timing wasn't a coincidence — it underscored exactly what critics of the unregulated market have been saying for years: when something goes wrong at a skill games location, there is no regulatory framework to protect anyone. Not the patron. Not the employee. And not the operator.

What Happened in Hazelton

A store clerk was fatally shot at a Hazelton, PA convenience store in 2020. The store hosted skill games machines, drew significant foot traffic, and operated without the surveillance, security, and oversight standards required of licensed Pennsylvania casinos. The lawsuit that followed argued — successfully — that the lack of regulation left employees and patrons exposed.

The jury agreed. The $15.3 million verdict wasn't about the skill games themselves being illegal. It was about what happens when high-cash, high-traffic operations run without the safety infrastructure that regulated gaming venues are required to maintain.

"The verdict highlights the vastly different approach between regulated casinos and skill games — and raises significant questions about whether to prohibit or at least regulate skill games in the same manner as the casino industry." — National Law Review, November 2025

Why This Is an Operator Problem, Not Just a Legal Abstraction

Pennsylvania's licensed casinos operate under a comprehensive regulatory framework managed by the Pennsylvania Gaming Control Board (PGCB). That framework mandates surveillance coverage, security staffing ratios, and incident reporting procedures. When something goes wrong at a regulated casino, the operator can point to documented compliance with those standards. It doesn't guarantee immunity, but it provides a defensible foundation.

Skill games operators currently have none of that framework — and that cuts both ways. It means lower operational costs today. It also means that if there's an incident at your location, your legal exposure is essentially unbounded. No one can point to a set of government-mandated safety standards you followed, because none exist for your machines.

With more than three times as many skill game machines in Pennsylvania as regulated slot machines, the aggregate liability exposure across the industry is enormous — and almost entirely unaddressed.

The Regulatory Gap as a Liability Gap

The comparison to regulated casinos isn't just an abstract policy debate. It has direct financial consequences for location owners and operators right now:

Factor Regulated PA Casino (PGCB) Current Skill Games Operator
Surveillance requirements Mandated by PGCB regulation None required
Security staffing Mandated minimums None required
Problem gambling protections Required signage, programs, self-exclusion No equivalent requirements
Incident reporting Mandatory to PGCB No required reporting structure
Liability in incident Compliance record provides partial defense No compliance record to reference

The irony is that the absence of regulation — which keeps your short-term costs down — is the same absence that leaves you exposed if an incident occurs. You can't point to a set of standards you followed if no standards exist.

What Smart Operators Are Doing Right Now

You can't create a PGCB licensing scheme on your own. But you can take practical steps to reduce your exposure while the regulatory situation gets sorted out:

  • Review your general liability and premises insurance coverage. Most policies have exclusions or sublimits for gaming operations. Confirm that your skill games machines are covered, and at what limit. A $15.3 million verdict is not a small business insurance event.
  • Install and maintain surveillance coverage around your machines. Even without a mandate, documented camera coverage demonstrates that you took reasonable precautions. It also changes the calculus for potential plaintiffs.
  • Document everything. Incident logs, maintenance records, any player interactions that raised concern. In an unregulated environment, your paper trail is the only trail you have.
  • Talk to an attorney familiar with gaming and premises liability before something happens. The Hazelton case took five years to reach a verdict. The liability clock starts the day of the incident, not the day the industry gets regulated.

What the Regulatory Debate Means for Your Liability Profile

Here's the scenario most operators aren't thinking about: the Pennsylvania Supreme Court rules in favor of the Commonwealth and skill games are classified as illegal gambling devices. Or Harrisburg passes a regulatory framework that grandfathers existing machines but imposes new compliance requirements retroactively.

In either scenario, your current operating practices — which were perfectly standard for an unregulated industry — may suddenly look like willful noncompliance in a future lawsuit. Courts don't always require that standards existed at the time of an incident. They ask what a reasonable operator should have known and done given the surrounding facts. Running a high-cash, high-traffic operation with no surveillance, no incident log, and no security review is harder to defend in 2026 than it was in 2020.

The absence of regulation is not the same as the absence of liability. It may actually make liability worse.

The Budget Deadline Adds Pressure — But Not the Right Kind

Pennsylvania's June 30 budget deadline has Harrisburg moving fast. Governor Shapiro's proposal includes $766 million in projected skill games revenue — which means some form of regulation is likely to pass this year. That's arguably good news for operators who want clarity. But there's a gap between "regulation passes" and "you're compliant," and that gap is where liability lives.

If a new regulatory framework passes in June with a 90-day compliance window and something happens at your location on day 60, you'll need to demonstrate that you were operating reasonably in the interim. The operators who are thinking about that now are the ones who won't be scrambling later.
